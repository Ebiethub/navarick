**Status:** Foundation / approval candidate

**Purpose:** Single source of truth for brand, experience, content, motion, and implementation.

## 0. Executive Direction

NAVARICK is a premium independent creative studio for ambitious organisations that understand design as a business asset. The site must make three ideas immediately legible: the studio has exceptional taste, it can execute across brand and digital, and it is a trustworthy partner for consequential work.

The experience is not a catalogue of services with fashionable effects attached. It is a controlled exhibition. Cinematic material, editorial typography, disciplined whitespace, and exact motion turn a small number of strong projects into evidence of judgement.

### North-star idea: **Form, revealed**

NAVARICK transforms unclear ambition into distinctive form. The visual metaphor is light moving across obsidian, bronze, and chrome glass: what first appears mysterious becomes precise. The signature hero interaction is a restrained material reveal of the NAVARICK object or wordmark. All subsequent motion supports narrative comprehension rather than competing with it.

### Primary objective

Generate qualified enquiries from founders, marketing leaders, and design-conscious organisations seeking premium brand identity, web design, web development, or UI/UX engagements.

### Experience promise

Within 30 seconds, a visitor should understand what NAVARICK does, feel its standard of craft, see credible proof, and know how to start a conversation.

### Non-goals

- Behave like a SaaS product, consultancy, production factory, or generalist agency.
- Maximise enquiry volume at the expense of lead quality.
- Showcase technology as spectacle without a narrative role.
- Depend on constant 3D, scroll-jacking, neon, or generic glass cards.
- Use vague agency language such as “we elevate brands” without evidence.

### Working assumptions

- V1 is English-language, desktop-first, and fully responsive down to 320 px.
- The supplied NAVARICK animation remains the primary art-direction reference; final implementation requires its production-ready source or a web-optimised derivative.
- Work is presented honestly. Unreleased or conceptual work is clearly labelled.
- The primary conversion is a project enquiry; email is the fallback.
- V1 launches with 4–6 strong projects and at least 2 complete case studies.
- A lightweight CMS may follow; V1 can use typed local content if publishing frequency is low.

# 1. Brand Strategy

## 1.1 Positioning

**Positioning statement**
For ambitious businesses at moments of creation or change, NAVARICK is a premium creative studio that unifies brand strategy, identity, digital design, and development into singular experiences—combining sharp thinking with uncommon craft from first idea to final interaction.

**Category:** Premium creative studio.
**Audience:** Founders, CEOs, CMOs, brand leaders, and product leaders who value distinction and are prepared to invest in it.
**Competitive advantage:** One coherent creative standard across identity, interface, and production—not a concept handed off between disconnected teams.
**Proof model:** Outcomes, process clarity, visual craft, client testimony, and finished work in use.

## 1.2 Mission

To turn ambitious ideas into distinctive brands and digital experiences that people recognise, trust, and remember.

## 1.3 Vision

To become the creative partner behind a small, influential body of brands that define their categories through clarity, character, and enduring design.

## 1.4 Brand personality

- **Discerning:** Edits rigorously; never decorates by default.
- **Assured:** Speaks with calm conviction, not bravado.
- **Inventive:** Finds a specific visual truth for every client.
- **Exacting:** Treats the final five percent as the work.
- **Human:** Intelligent and direct without becoming cold or aloof.

## 1.5 Brand attributes

| Core | Supporting | Avoid |
|---|---|---|
| Precise | Cinematic | Flashy |
| Distinctive | Tactile | Trend-chasing |
| Refined | Progressive | Sterile |
| Trustworthy | Editorial | Corporate |
| Enduring | Selective | Elitist |

## 1.6 Value proposition

NAVARICK gives clients a coherent creative partner from strategic foundation to shipped digital experience. The result is not merely a better-looking business, but a clearer position, a stronger first impression, and a more consistent experience across every important touchpoint.

## 1.7 Messaging hierarchy

1. **Promise:** Distinctive brands, precisely made.
2. **Capability:** Brand identity, web design, development, and UI/UX under one creative direction.
3. **Method:** Discover the truth, define the system, design the expression, build the experience.
4. **Proof:** Selected work, measurable outcomes, client voices, and final artefacts.
5. **Action:** Bring us the ambition; we will help give it form.

### Recommended hero copy

**Eyebrow:** Independent creative studio
**Headline:** We give ambitious ideas distinctive form.
**Support:** NAVARICK builds identities and digital experiences where strategy, design, and technology move as one.
**Primary CTA:** View selected work
**Secondary CTA:** Start a project

Alternative campaign line: **Built to be recognised. Made to endure.**

## 1.8 Tone of voice

### Principles

- **Clear before clever:** Make the point; then make it memorable.
- **Specific over inflated:** Name the decision, deliverable, or outcome.
- **Compact and composed:** Short sentences, confident pacing, generous pauses.
- **Expert without theatre:** Explain reasoning plainly; avoid jargon as status display.
- **Inviting, not pleading:** CTAs are calm invitations.

### Voice examples

| Instead of | Use |
|---|---|
| We craft transformative solutions | We build identities people can recognise in an instant. |
| Full-service digital innovation | One creative direction, from strategy to shipped website. |
| Let’s create magic together | Tell us what you are building. |
| Cutting-edge experiences | Fast, accessible, carefully engineered experiences. |

### Editorial rules

- Use sentence case in UI and most headings.
- Prefer “studio” to “agency.”
- Use “we” sparingly and lead with client value or work.
- Avoid superlatives unless substantiated.
- Headlines: 3–10 words. Body paragraphs: usually under 70 words.

# 2. Design System

## 2.1 Visual principles

1. **Darkness creates focus.** Black is spatial, not merely a theme.
2. **Material creates memory.** Chrome glass and bronze appear as controlled highlights.
3. **Type carries authority.** Editorial scale does more work than decoration.
4. **Work remains the colour.** Project media is not forced through a brand tint.
5. **Restraint signals confidence.** One hero spectacle; quiet supporting sections.

## 2.2 Colour system

### Core palette

| Token | HEX | Use |
|---|---:|---|
| `obsidian-950` | `#070706` | Primary canvas, hero, page transitions |
| `obsidian-900` | `#0D0D0C` | Raised dark surfaces |
| `charcoal-800` | `#181816` | Cards, menus, alternate sections |
| `charcoal-700` | `#24221F` | Hover surfaces and strong borders |
| `bronze-500` | `#A8733A` | Primary accent, active details |
| `bronze-400` | `#C08A4A` | Warm highlight, hover accent |
| `champagne-300` | `#D8C29A` | Premium highlight, selected text |
| `champagne-200` | `#E7D8BB` | Fine rules and restrained glow |
| `chrome-300` | `#BFC3C5` | Chrome-glass edges and metadata |
| `chrome-100` | `#E3E5E5` | Specular highlight only |
| `soft-white` | `#F2F0EB` | Primary text on dark |
| `muted-gray` | `#9A9892` | Secondary text; large text only |
| `quiet-gray` | `#696762` | Decorative labels, disabled states |
| `signal-red` | `#C45A4A` | Errors only |
| `signal-green` | `#78906B` | Success only |

### Usage ratios

- 78% obsidian/charcoal foundations.
- 16% white, gray, and natural project media.
- 5% bronze/champagne.
- 1% chrome specular detail.

Gold is punctuation, never wallpaper. Gradients are permitted only to simulate physical light on material; never use a generic gold-to-purple decorative gradient.

### Contrast requirements

- Normal body text: WCAG AA, minimum 4.5:1.
- Large display text: minimum 3:1.
- `muted-gray` is not used below 18 px on `obsidian-950` without a contrast check.
- Focus, validation, and active states never rely on colour alone.

## 2.3 Typography

### Recommended family set

| Role | Font | Fallback | Character |
|---|---|---|---|
| Primary display | **Instrument Serif** | `Georgia, serif` | Editorial tension, luxury, expressive italics |
| Heading / UI | **Neue Montreal** | `Inter, Arial, sans-serif` | Modern, neutral, confident |
| Body / functional | **Inter** | `Arial, sans-serif` | Highly legible, complete UI range |

**Licensing note:** Neue Montreal requires an appropriate commercial licence. If unavailable, use **Manrope** for headings and UI. Instrument Serif and Inter can be self-hosted from licensed/open font files to avoid render delay and improve privacy.

### Type scale

| Token | Desktop | Mobile | Line height | Use |
|---|---:|---:|---:|---|
| `display-xl` | clamp(72px, 10vw, 160px) | 54–76px | 0.88–0.94 | Hero only |
| `display-lg` | clamp(56px, 7.5vw, 112px) | 44–60px | 0.94 | Page titles |
| `heading-1` | 64–80px | 40–48px | 1.00 | Section statements |
| `heading-2` | 40–52px | 32–38px | 1.05 | Section headings |
| `heading-3` | 26–32px | 24–28px | 1.15 | Cards |
| `body-lg` | 20px | 18px | 1.50 | Lead copy |
| `body` | 16px | 16px | 1.60 | Standard copy |
| `small` | 14px | 14px | 1.45 | Metadata |
| `label` | 12px | 12px | 1.20 | Uppercase labels, +0.12em tracking |

Display serif should appear selectively—typically one expressive phrase within a sans-serif composition. Do not set long paragraphs, form UI, or all headings in serif.

## 2.4 Spacing

Base unit: **4 px**. Primary scale: 4, 8, 12, 16, 24, 32, 48, 64, 96, 128, 160, 240.

- Section padding desktop: 128–200 px vertical.
- Section padding tablet: 96–128 px.
- Section padding mobile: 72–96 px.
- Component gaps: 12–32 px.
- Text measure: 45–72 characters for body; lead copy max 840 px.
- Whitespace is structural and should not be backfilled with decoration.

## 2.5 Layout grid

| Viewport | Columns | Gutter | Margin | Max content width |
|---|---:|---:|---:|---:|
| ≥1440 px | 12 | 24 px | 48–64 px | 1600 px |
| 1024–1439 px | 12 | 20 px | 32 px | none |
| 768–1023 px | 8 | 20 px | 24 px | none |
| 320–767 px | 4 | 16 px | 20 px | none |

Use an occasional controlled full-bleed media break. Text and controls return to the grid. Optical alignment may override mathematical alignment for the wordmark, oversized type, and circular controls.

## 2.6 Shape and borders

- Default radius: 0 px for editorial media and section frames.
- Small radius: 4 px for inputs and utility surfaces.
- Medium radius: 10 px for menus and compact cards.
- Pill: 999 px only for tags, small CTAs, and the floating navigation.
- Borders: 1 px `rgba(242,240,235,.12)`; strong borders `.22`.
- Avoid nested rounded cards and large “friendly SaaS” radii.

## 2.7 Shadows and depth

Dark interfaces gain depth from tonal separation and highlights, not large gray shadows.

- `shadow-float`: `0 18px 60px rgba(0,0,0,.42)`.
- `shadow-close`: `0 8px 24px rgba(0,0,0,.28)`.
- `glow-bronze`: `0 0 48px rgba(168,115,58,.14)`; reserved for active material.
- `inset-highlight`: `inset 0 1px 0 rgba(255,255,255,.12)`.

## 2.8 Chrome-glass treatment

Glass is a rare material, used for the navigation, selected controls, and the signature 3D object—not every content card.

```css
.glass {
  background: rgba(24, 24, 22, 0.52);
  border: 1px solid rgba(227, 229, 229, 0.14);
  box-shadow: inset 0 1px 0 rgba(255,255,255,.10), 0 18px 60px rgba(0,0,0,.32);
  backdrop-filter: blur(18px) saturate(115%);
}
```

Use a 1 px top/left highlight falloff to suggest thickness. Never place low-contrast body copy directly on moving glass.

## 2.9 Imagery and art direction

- Prefer authored, high-resolution client work, close crops, material detail, and real-world application.
- Mix hero scale, full compositions, and intimate details to create rhythm.
- Mockups should serve context; flat design artefacts should prove craft.
- Avoid stock-office imagery, laptop mockup repetition, gratuitous device frames, and identical aspect ratios.
- Grade NAVARICK-owned editorial imagery toward neutral blacks and warm highlights; do not recolour client work.

## 2.10 Motion tokens

| Token | Value | Use |
|---|---:|---|
| `instant` | 120 ms | Press/active feedback |
| `fast` | 220 ms | Hover and small UI |
| `base` | 420 ms | Component entrances |
| `slow` | 700 ms | Media reveal |
| `cinematic` | 1100 ms | Hero and page transition |
| `ease-out` | `cubic-bezier(.16,1,.3,1)` | Primary reveal |
| `ease-in-out` | `cubic-bezier(.65,0,.35,1)` | Morph and crossfade |

No spring bounce in primary brand motion. Stagger words by 35–60 ms and groups by 80–120 ms. Motion must be interruptible, transform/opacity-led, and respectful of `prefers-reduced-motion`.

## 2.11 Responsive and accessibility rules

- Maintain hierarchy; do not merely shrink the desktop composition.
- Replace pointer-dependent interactions with an authored static or tap-based state.
- Use `100dvh` for full-screen mobile sections and account for safe areas.
- Maintain a 44×44 px minimum interactive target.
- Provide visible keyboard focus in champagne or soft white with offset.
- All decorative canvases are hidden from assistive technology; content remains semantic HTML.
- Videos are muted, captioned if informational, pauseable where necessary, and have posters.

# 4. Homepage Experience

## 4.1 Preloader / opening frame

- **Purpose:** Protect the first cinematic frame while critical type and hero media initialise.
- **Content:** NAVARICK wordmark, minimal progress line or three-state material shimmer.
- **Layout:** Full viewport obsidian field; wordmark centred; status at lower edge.
- **Interaction:** None. Skip after assets are cached; never block longer than 2.5 seconds.
- **Animation:** Wordmark resolves from low contrast; transition wipes or iris-reveals into the same spatial position in the hero.
- **Conversion goal:** None directly; establish quality without delaying access.

## 4.2 Hero — Form, revealed

- **Purpose:** Establish category, attitude, and signature memory in one viewport.
- **Content:** Eyebrow, headline, one-sentence positioning, View work, Start a project, optional availability note.
- **Layout:** `100svh`/`100dvh`; full-bleed NAVARICK object/animation centred. Headline occupies upper/middle grid; supporting copy and actions anchor bottom corners. Media gets the focal point, not a card.
- **Interaction:** Desktop cursor subtly changes the key light or reveal mask within a bounded area. Touch receives a slow authored light pass. Scroll cue is quiet and semantic.
- **Animation:** Background arrives first; chrome form emerges through bronze side-light; headline reveals by word; support and controls follow. Total opening sequence 1.6–2.2 seconds after readiness. No looping text animation.
- **Conversion goal:** Drive visitors to selected work while preserving a direct enquiry path.

## 4.3 Selected work — Evidence at scale

- **Purpose:** Prove taste and relevance immediately.
- **Content:** 3–4 flagship projects with title, sector, disciplines, year, one-line result, and strong cover media.
- **Layout:** Alternating full-width editorial frames. First project nearly full viewport; subsequent projects vary between 7/5 and 5/7 grid compositions. Avoid a uniform card grid on Home.
- **Interaction:** Hover shifts media crop 1–2%, reveals metadata, and draws an arrow/rule. Entire title/media group is linked with explicit accessible label.
- **Animation:** Clip or curtain image reveal; metadata fades only after image is legible. Optional sticky project progression, limited to this section.
- **Conversion goal:** Open a case study and move from aesthetic interest to evidence.

## 4.4 Studio proposition — One practice, one standard

- **Purpose:** Explain the integrated offer and point of view.
- **Content:** “From first principle to final pixel.” Short statement plus four disciplines.
- **Layout:** Oversized statement on 8 columns; services indexed beneath using precise horizontal rules. One serif phrase adds contrast.
- **Interaction:** Hover/focus on a discipline reveals a relevant artefact crop or concise deliverable list; no accordion dependency for core information.
- **Animation:** Line-by-line text reveal with low vertical travel; rules draw from left to right.
- **Conversion goal:** Route visitors to Services or reassure multidisciplinary buyers.

## 4.5 Capabilities — What we make

- **Purpose:** Make scope concrete and allow self-qualification.
- **Content:** Four numbered services, each with outcome statement and key deliverables.
- **Layout:** Large editorial list: number, service, short description, deliverables. Optional single media stage changes per active row.
- **Interaction:** Focus/hover activates one row and crossfades the media stage; rows link to anchored service details.
- **Animation:** Controlled 350–500 ms crossfade; no horizontal carousels for essential content.
- **Conversion goal:** Move qualified visitors to service detail or enquiry.

## 4.6 Featured case-study interlude — The transformation

- **Purpose:** Demonstrate process and measurable change, not just output.
- **Content:** One before/after comparison, challenge, key decision, and result.
- **Layout:** Full-bleed transformation canvas followed by a compact three-column fact row.
- **Interaction:** Drag/keyboard-accessible comparison only when the media truly supports it; otherwise use sequenced static frames.
- **Animation:** A slow vertical or radial reveal connects before and after. Reduced motion shows both side by side.
- **Conversion goal:** Establish strategic depth and trust.

## 4.7 Process — Clarity without bureaucracy

- **Purpose:** Reduce perceived project risk.
- **Content:** Discover, Define, Design, Deliver; responsibilities, outputs, and decision gates.
- **Layout:** Four chapters in a horizontal desktop grid or vertically pinned sequence; plain vertical stack on mobile.
- **Interaction:** Scroll progress marks the active chapter; each remains readable without interaction.
- **Animation:** One bronze rule advances through the stages; content fades rather than flying in.
- **Conversion goal:** Make starting feel structured and safe.

## 4.8 Client voice / trust

- **Purpose:** Add external validation at the moment of consideration.
- **Content:** One strong 25–45 word testimonial, person, role, organisation, plus a restrained client list or outcome facts.
- **Layout:** Quote at large scale on 8–10 columns; attribution and proof aligned on the remaining columns.
- **Interaction:** If multiple quotes exist, use direct controls—not autoplay.
- **Animation:** Soft crossfade and line reveal; no rotating carousel timer.
- **Conversion goal:** Resolve doubt before the enquiry invitation.

## 4.9 About teaser — The studio behind the work

- **Purpose:** Humanise NAVARICK and communicate working style.
- **Content:** A concise studio philosophy, founder/team image or tactile working detail, and link to About.
- **Layout:** Asymmetric 5/7 composition; candid image counterbalances formal typography.
- **Interaction:** Subtle image parallax (maximum 4%) and magnetic text-link treatment on capable pointers.
- **Animation:** Image and copy enter on separate but coordinated timings.
- **Conversion goal:** Build affinity and route visitors seeking cultural fit.

## 4.10 Closing invitation — Bring us the ambition

- **Purpose:** Turn accumulated confidence into action.
- **Content:** “Have something worth making unmistakable?” Start a project, email, and expected reply time.
- **Layout:** Near-full viewport with oversized type; bronze/champagne material highlight returns to close the visual loop.
- **Interaction:** CTA has a subtle magnetic pull and text/arrow swap; works identically by keyboard without movement dependency.
- **Animation:** Ambient light pass once when entering viewport; no continuous glow loop.
- **Conversion goal:** Start a qualified enquiry.

## 4.11 Footer

- **Purpose:** Provide utility, legitimacy, and a clean final route.
- **Content:** Wordmark, navigation, email, social links, location/time zone if useful, legal, copyright.
- **Layout:** Dense but calm 12-column index; oversized cropped wordmark may anchor the bottom edge.
- **Interaction:** Simple underline/rule states.
- **Animation:** Minimal; footer should feel settled.
- **Conversion goal:** Recover navigation and direct-contact intent.

# 5. Portfolio & Case-study System

## 5.1 Work listing

The Work page is an exhibition index, not a masonry dump. Start with a concise curatorial statement and a featured project, followed by a variable editorial grid.

Each project preview includes:

- Project title and client.
- Sector, disciplines, year.
- A result-led one-line synopsis.
- Landscape or portrait cover with intentional art direction.
- Availability state only where relevant: Live, In progress, Concept.

Filtering should appear only when the catalogue exceeds eight projects. Until then, visible category labels and a well-ordered list outperform filter UI. On hover, show useful information—not an opaque black overlay with the title repeated.

## 5.2 Case-study narrative flow

1. **Opening statement:** Client, transformation, disciplines, year, live URL where permitted.
2. **Hero artefact:** The most compelling expression, not automatically a laptop mockup.
3. **Context:** What changed, who it affected, and why the work mattered.
4. **Challenge:** Specific constraints, tensions, or broken perceptions.
5. **Strategic idea:** The central decision that organised the work.
6. **Identity system:** Mark, typography, colour, composition, voice, motion.
7. **Digital experience:** Information architecture, interface logic, responsive behaviour, development craft.
8. **Application:** The system in credible environments and multiple scales.
9. **Before / after:** Show the contrast in perception, clarity, or usability.
10. **Outcome:** Qualitative and quantitative results; label unavailable metrics honestly.
11. **Client voice:** Short, attributable testimony.
12. **Credits:** NAVARICK role, collaborators, client team, photography/type licensing.
13. **Next project:** Large visual handoff that sustains browsing.

## 5.3 Before / after rules

- Compare equivalent items, crops, and contexts.
- Explain what changed and why; do not depend on visual judgement alone.
- For branding, compare recognition, hierarchy, and system coherence.
- For digital, compare task flow, responsiveness, accessibility, and performance.
- Use draggable comparison only when it adds evidence and remains keyboard accessible.
- Never manufacture weak “before” work to exaggerate the result.

## 5.4 Visual storytelling rhythm

Use a repeating narrative cadence:

**Statement → hero image → detail → explanation → system → application → result.**

Alternate visual density. A full-bleed frame should be followed by a quieter explanatory passage. Use captions for design decisions and alt text for content, not decorative prose. Every image must either prove a decision, show scale, demonstrate coherence, or create an intentional emotional pause.

## 5.5 Mockup presentation

- Use 30–40% real-world or dimensional mockups, 60–70% direct artefact and interface evidence.
- Build custom scenes from project materials rather than recycled device templates.
- Show responsive sites as behaviour across widths, not three floating screens.
- Use video for motion identity and interaction; provide poster, controls where needed, and compressed fallbacks.
- Keep shadows and environment lighting consistent with each case study, not forcibly NAVARICK-branded.
- Target AVIF/WebP for stills and H.264/WebM where browser support and encoding quality justify dual sources.

## 5.6 Case-study content schema

```ts

  slug: string;
  title: string;
  client: string;
  year: number;
  sector: string;
  disciplines: string[];
  summary: string;
  challenge: RichText;
  strategicIdea: RichText;
  outcomes: { value: string; label: string; source?: string }[];
  hero: Media;
  chapters: CaseStudyChapter[];
  testimonial?: Testimonial;
  credits: Credit[];
  seo: SeoFields;
};
```

# 6. Motion Language

## 6.1 Character

Motion is **weighted, optical, and inevitable**. It behaves like a camera, a piece of material catching light, or a page being composed—not like a playful UI toy.

### Signature mechanic

The only dominant signature mechanic is the hero material reveal. Cursor input changes a bounded light/mask field with a damped follow (`lerp` approximately 0.08–0.12). It never rotates the object wildly or obscures the proposition. Touch uses a static centre reveal or one slow autonomous pass.

## 6.2 Hero behaviour

1. Obsidian field appears immediately.
2. Hero media settles with a 1–2% zoom-out or light sweep.
3. NAVARICK form catches a bronze edge and chrome highlight.
4. Headline reveals word-by-word with 35–60 ms staggering.
5. Support and CTAs rise 12–20 px and fade in.
6. Interaction becomes available only after the entrance completes.

## 6.3 Scroll animation

- Use Lenis only for subtle interpolation; preserve native scroll semantics and user control.
- Trigger content 10–18% inside the viewport, once by default.
- Parallax ranges stay under 8% of element height; 3–5% is preferred.
- Sticky storytelling is limited to selected work and one process/case-study passage.
- Never lock scrolling to animation completion.

## 6.4 Text reveals

- Headlines: words or lines, clipped vertically or blurred from 6 px to 0.
- Body copy: paragraph-level fade/translate, never character stagger.
- Keep readable DOM text intact; split visual clones must be `aria-hidden`.
- Avoid repeated reveals when the user scrolls back.

## 6.5 Image reveals

- Primary: mask/clip reveal paired with a subtle 1.03 → 1.00 media scale.
- Secondary: crossfade for media-state changes.
- Avoid every image entering from a different direction.
- Do not animate layout dimensions or introduce cumulative layout shift.

## 6.6 Hover interactions

- Links: underline/rule draw, colour shift, or arrow translation of 4–6 px.
- Project cards: crop shift ≤2%, metadata reveal, no card lift that breaks alignment.
- Buttons: subtle fill inversion; magnetic motion capped at 6–10 px on fine pointers.
- Cursor treatment, if used, is decorative and never the only indication of clickability.

## 6.7 Page transitions

- 700–1100 ms total, using an obsidian curtain, shared project media where reliable, or controlled fade.
- New route title/media becomes visible before the outgoing layer fully clears.
- Navigation remains responsive; transition cancels safely on rapid route changes.
- Disable elaborate transitions for reduced motion and when browser back/forward restoration would be harmed.

## 6.8 Reduced-motion mode

- Remove smooth scrolling, parallax, cursor tracking, blur animation, and long route transitions.
- Retain short opacity fades under 160 ms or render final states immediately.
- Show a polished hero poster and complete headline from first paint.
- Never hide content behind animation initial states when JavaScript fails.

## 6.9 Performance guardrails

- No more than one persistent WebGL canvas on a route.
- Pause canvas/video when offscreen or the tab is hidden.
- Animate transform and opacity; use filter/masks within measured bounds.
- Target stable 55–60 fps on modern desktop and 30+ fps on representative mid-tier mobile.
- Provide static fallbacks below a tested capability threshold.

# 8. Technical Architecture

## 8.1 Recommended stack

### Next.js (App Router)

Use for routing, React Server Components, metadata, image/font optimisation, static generation of portfolio pages, route-level loading/error states, and server-side form handling. Keep most editorial pages server-rendered; introduce client boundaries only around actual interaction.

### TypeScript

Use strict mode for content models, component contracts, animation configuration, form payloads, and analytics events. Case-study content must fail validation during build rather than fail silently in production.

### Tailwind CSS

Use for tokens, responsive composition, layout primitives, and small state styles. Define NAVARICK colours, spacing, typography, easing, and z-index in a semantic theme. Use CSS modules or colocated CSS for complex masks, pseudo-elements, and canvas-adjacent effects; do not turn long animation timelines into unreadable utility strings.

### GSAP + ScrollTrigger

Use narrowly for deterministic timeline choreography, pinned case-study sequences, complex mask movement, and the hero light/material timeline. Centralise registration and lifecycle cleanup. Do not use GSAP for ordinary hover states or every viewport fade.

### Motion for React (Framer Motion)

Use for route transitions, component entrances, layout/shared-element transitions where stable, and small interactive states. Standardise variants and easing tokens. Avoid running GSAP and Motion on the same transform property of one element.

### React Three Fiber

Use only for the signature NAVARICK material object if a true 3D source asset exists and WebGL adds visible value. Keep the canvas isolated, dynamically loaded, quality-scaled by device pixel ratio, and backed by a static image/video poster. If the source reference is already a rendered animation, prefer optimised video over recreating 3D for its own sake.

### Lenis

Use as a progressive enhancement for subtle smooth-scroll interpolation and GSAP synchronisation on capable devices. Disable for reduced motion, touch contexts where it degrades behaviour, and browser restoration. Native scrolling remains the functional baseline.

## 8.2 Responsibility matrix

| Concern | Primary tool | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| Routing, content, SEO | Next.js | Server-first |
| Contracts and content schema | TypeScript + Zod | Validate at build/form boundary |
| Layout and tokens | Tailwind + CSS | Semantic tokens |
| Simple UI motion | CSS / Motion | 120–420 ms |
| Route and layout transitions | Motion | Reduced-motion fallback |
| Hero/pinned timelines | GSAP | One owner per transform |
| Signature 3D material | R3F | Optional, lazy, poster fallback |
| Scroll interpolation | Lenis | Progressive enhancement |

## 8.4 Content architecture

Start with typed MDX or TypeScript content when the studio team is small and updates are infrequent. Move to a headless CMS only when non-technical publishing, draft preview, or frequent project updates justify its operational cost. All content should map to a stable `CaseStudy` schema so storage can change without rewriting presentation components.

## 8.5 Contact flow

Recommended fields: name, email, company, project type, short project description, desired timing, and optional investment range. Keep the form to one page with clear progress-free completion. Validate client and server side, rate-limit, use a honeypot or managed bot protection, avoid logging sensitive free text, and send both studio notification and visitor confirmation.

## 8.6 SEO and discoverability

- Unique metadata and social image per case study.
- Organisation/ProfessionalService and CreativeWork structured data where accurate.
- Semantic headings, descriptive titles, canonical URLs, sitemap, robots file.
- Indexable project narrative—not images alone.
- Alt text describes the artefact and relevant context; decorative material imagery uses empty alt.

## 8.7 Analytics

Track only decisions that improve the experience:

- `view_project`
- `view_case_study_depth` at meaningful chapters
- `select_service`
- `start_inquiry`
- `submit_inquiry`
- `inquiry_error`
- `contact_email_click`
- `external_project_visit`

Use privacy-conscious analytics and document consent requirements before launch. Never use heatmaps/session recording by default on enquiry-form content.

## 8.8 Performance budgets

| Metric | Target |
|---|---:|
| LCP (75th percentile, mobile) | ≤2.5 s |
| INP (75th percentile) | ≤200 ms |
| CLS | ≤0.05 |
| Initial route JS, gzipped | ≤170 KB excluding optional hero 3D |
| Initial hero media | ≤2.5 MB desktop / ≤1.2 MB mobile |
| Font files above the fold | ≤180 KB total |

Use responsive media, preload only the actual LCP asset, subset fonts, dynamically import WebGL, and measure the real production build on representative hardware.

## 8.9 Testing strategy

- **Unit:** schema validation, utilities, form validation, motion preference logic.
- **Component:** interactive navigation, form states, before/after keyboard controls.
- **End-to-end:** core navigation, project browsing, enquiry success/failure, keyboard path.
- **Visual regression:** Home, Work, one case study, Contact at 320, 375, 768, 1024, and 1440 px.
- **Accessibility:** automated axe plus manual keyboard, screen reader spot checks, contrast, reduced motion.
- **Performance:** Lighthouse CI and production Web Vitals; test canvas/video fallbacks on mid-tier mobile.

## 8.11 Engineering boundaries

### Always

- Preserve semantic content without JavaScript or motion.
- Validate content and forms at system boundaries.
- Clean up observers, animation frames, listeners, timers, and GSAP contexts.
- Test responsive behaviour, keyboard navigation, and reduced motion.
- Credit collaborators and licence fonts/assets correctly.

### Ask first

- Add a CMS, analytics vendor, form/email service, or new paid dependency.
- Change the public URL structure or case-study schema after content entry begins.
- Add persistent cookies, session recording, or third-party embeds.
- Replace the supplied hero asset or alter the NAVARICK mark.

### Never

- Commit secrets or client-confidential assets.
- fabricate client results, testimonials, awards, or project status.
- Ship a WebGL-only hero without fallback.
- Hide navigation or content behind a motion sequence.
- use smooth scroll to override accessibility or browser history behaviour.

# 10. Success Criteria

## Experience acceptance criteria

- A first-time visitor can identify NAVARICK as a brand and digital creative studio from the hero alone.
- Selected work appears within the first two meaningful scroll sections.
- Every service is understandable in terms of outcome and deliverables.
- Every live case study includes context, a strategic idea, system evidence, outcome, and credits.
- Primary enquiry access is present on every route within one interaction.
- The site remains complete and composed with reduced motion enabled.
- All core journeys work by keyboard and pass agreed WCAG 2.2 AA checks.
- No motion causes scroll trapping, content loss, horizontal overflow, or layout shift.
- Production performance meets the budgets in §8.8 at the 75th percentile where field data is available.

## Business indicators

Measure quality before raw volume:

- Qualified enquiry completion rate.
- Percentage of enquiries aligned to target services and investment.
- Case-study views per engaged session.
- Case-study completion depth.
- Enquiry starts from case studies versus generic pages.
- Client-reported relevance of new leads after 60–90 days.

No numeric conversion target should be invented before baseline traffic and enquiry quality are known. Establish a 30-day baseline, then set improvement targets.

# 11. Delivery Phases

## Phase 1 — Foundation

Approve positioning, visual system, sitemap, content schema, hero asset approach, and this document.

## Phase 2 — Content and wireframes

Write project narratives; assemble assets; produce low-fidelity flows for all routes and responsive states; validate enquiry fields.

## Phase 3 — Visual design

Design Home, Work, case-study system, Services, About, Contact, mobile menu, system states, and reduced-motion variants. Prototype only the signature interactions necessary to judge timing.

## Phase 4 — Build

Create the server-first Next.js foundation, tokens, components, content system, forms, and progressive motion layers. Add 3D only after the static/media experience meets the quality bar.

## Phase 5 — Quality and launch

Perform content, responsive, browser, accessibility, motion, performance, SEO, analytics, and form-delivery checks. Run a soft launch, observe real behaviour, then tune.

# 12. Risks & Mitigations

| Risk | Effect | Mitigation |
|---|---|---|
| Hero 3D becomes the project | Slow launch, weak content | Time-box prototype; use video/poster fallback |
| Too few strong case studies | Portfolio feels decorative | Prioritise narrative depth over project count |
| Gold/glass overuse | Crypto or template aesthetic | Enforce usage ratios and component limits |
| Motion harms usability | Friction, poor mobile experience | Progressive enhancement, motion budgets, reduced mode |
| Font licensing is unresolved | Legal/visual rework | Confirm licence before visual-design sign-off |
| Generic agency copy | Weak differentiation | Require specific decisions, outputs, and evidence |
| Heavy client media | Poor Core Web Vitals | Asset pipeline, responsive formats, per-route budgets |

# 14. Creative Quality Gate

Before a design or build is accepted, ask:

- Is the idea recognisably NAVARICK without the logo?
- Does the work lead while the interface recedes?
- Is there one memorable interaction rather than many fashionable ones?
- Does every material, motion, and line of copy have a job?
- Would removing an effect make the communication clearer? If yes, remove it.
- Does mobile feel deliberately art-directed?
- Is the experience still excellent when motion, canvas, or video is unavailable?
- Is every claim supported by visible work or credible evidence?

If those answers hold, the site will feel premium for the right reason: not because it performs luxury, but because it demonstrates judgement.
